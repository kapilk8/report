                                       Abstract
  Property tax collection in most Indian municipalities remains a manual and inef-
ficient process, characterized by numerous administrative loopholes. Many smaller
urban local bodies continue to rely on outdated assessment methods and property
data that lack accuracy. For citizens, the lack of transparency in tax calculation and
the cumbersome payment process present significant hurdles. Conversely, municipal
staff allocate a disproportionate amount of time to revenue collection and enforce-
ment, detracting from the delivery of essential public services.

  The Smart Property Tax Management System (SMPTMS) addresses these chal-
lenges, specifically for Jaipur Nagar Nigam. We have developed a comprehensive
web-based application that automates the entire tax cycle: property registration, tax
assessment, online payments, and receipt generation. The platform allows citizens
to register their properties, view their calculated tax based on plot area, zone, and
land use, and complete payments through a secure gateway—eliminating the need
for manual intervention and frequent visits to municipal offices.

  The system features a dashboard for municipal officials, incorporating a live map
(developed using Leaflet.js) that displays all registered properties in Jaipur, catego-
rized by payment status. The administrative interface provides advanced analytics,
tools for identifying tax defaulters, and reporting features designed to optimize rev-
enue management.

  Developed using Next.js 14, TypeScript, Supabase (PostgreSQL), Tailwind CSS,
and Razorpay, the SMPTMS is a cost-effective and transparent solution for mu-
nicipal tax management. The calculation methodology—Tax = (Plot Area x DLC
Rate)/2000—ensures simplicity and transparency. The implementation of SMPTMS
demonstrates the potential for digital transformation in municipal tax administration,
even in medium-sized cities like Jaipur.

Chapter 1
Introduction

1.1    Problem Statement and Objective

  The property tax system in Jaipur currently faces significant challenges, primar-
ily due to its reliance on outdated manual processes. A large number of properties
remain unregistered as a result of the lack of a streamlined digital registration mech-
anism. Tax assessments are often inconsistent, influenced by antiquated records
or subjective administrative decisions. The lack of transparency in tax calculation
methods has fostered public distrust. Furthermore, municipal personnel spend ex-
cessive time on revenue collection and enforcement, diverting resources from the
improvement of public services.
      Our project aims to address these issues by implementing a digital platform
that facilitates property registration, tax assessment, online payments, and receipt
generation. This centralized system provides a transparent and efficient interface for
both citizens and municipal staff, reducing administrative overhead and eliminating
the need for frequent office visits.
      India’s property tax collection is approximately 0.2% of GDP, which is sig-
nificantly lower than in developed economies such as the United States or Canada,
where property tax revenue can account for 3–4% of GDP. This shortfall impacts the
development of basic infrastructure, as Urban Local Bodies (ULBs) depend on these
revenues to fund essential services such as roads, sanitation, and water supply.


1.2    Literature Survey / Market Survey

  Larger Indian metropolitan areas, such as Pune (PCMC) and Bangalore (BBMP),
have demonstrated significant success with digital property tax systems, enhancing
tax compliance and reducing administrative burdens. However, these platforms are

often complex, costly, and specifically designed for major urban centers. Conse-
quently, there is a pressing need for a more streamlined and cost-effective solution
tailored to the requirements of cities like Jaipur, which may have limited adminis-
trative resources.
      International experience demonstrates that the integration of tax management
systems with digital mapping provides tangible benefits. For instance, the Valuation
Office Agency in the UK and county-level GIS systems in the US facilitate efficient
property discovery, accurate valuation, and data-driven planning. The SMPTMS in-
corporates key features from these established models, adapted to the specific needs
of the Jaipur municipality.
      Academic research has consistently identified three primary issues in India’s
property tax administration: outdated or unmapped records, inconsistent tax calcu-
lation methodologies, and a lack of public trust, which adversely affects voluntary
compliance.


1.3    Introduction to Project

  The SMPTMS provides citizens with a streamlined dashboard for property reg-
istration, enabling them to input essential details such as plot number, area, zone,
and land use category. The system then automatically calculates the due tax based
on the applicable District Level Committee (DLC) rates. Upon confirmation of the
assessed tax, users can complete the payment securely through the integrated Ra-
zorpay gateway and download an official digital receipt, eliminating the need for
in-person administrative visits.
      Administrators are provided with a dedicated control panel featuring a live GIS
map, which visualizes all registered properties in Jaipur through color-coded status
indicators. The administrative interface enables officials to identify tax defaulters,
manage property registrations, update DLC rates by zone, and generate compre-
hensive reports filtered by ward or zone. The SMPTMS is deployed and accessi-
ble at smptms.vercel.app, utilizing a Supabase PostgreSQL backend integrated with
authentic municipal data from Jaipur. The system offers a robust solution to the

complexities of traditional property tax administration, emphasizing transparency,
efficiency, and data-driven decision-making.


1.4    Proposed Logic / Algorithm / Business Plan / Solution

  The solution is structured around a straightforward end-to-end workflow. A prop-
erty owner registers on the platform and submits property details including plot num-
ber, area in square metres, zone category, and land use type (residential, commercial,
or industrial). The backend calculates annual tax automatically using the formula:

                    Annual Tax = (Plot Area × DLC Rate) / 2000

      The DLC rate is configured per zone by the admin and reflects the government’s
published District Level Circle rates for Jaipur. Once the tax amount is displayed,
the citizen proceeds to payment through Razorpay’s hosted checkout. On successful
payment, Razorpay sends a webhook callback to the Next.js API server, which ver-
ifies the payment signature cryptographically before updating the property status in
Supabase and generating a downloadable PDF receipt.
      On the administrative side, all registered properties are fetched from Supabase
and rendered as markers on a Leaflet.js map centred on Jaipur coordinates. Green
markers represent paid properties and orange markers represent pending ones. Ad-
mins can filter by ward, zone, or payment status, click any marker to view full prop-
erty details, and export zone-wise tax reports for departmental use.
      The three-agent architecture separates the authentication module, tax calcula-
tion engine, and payment processing module into independent, maintainable units
— making the system robust and easy to extend.


1.5    Scope of the Project

  SMPTMS covers property tax administration specifically for Jaipur Nagar Nigam.
The system’s functional scope includes property registration, automated DLC-rate-
based tax calculation, online payment processing via Razorpay, digital receipt gen-
eration, GIS-based property mapping with Leaflet.js, and administrative reporting

Department of Computer Science and Engineering (AI), SKIT, Jaipur                    3
with zone-wise and ward-wise filters.
     The current version does not cover other municipal revenue streams such as
trade licenses, water bills, or building plan approvals. It is not intended to replace
state-level platforms like Rajasthan’s ULBHRMS but to function as a lightweight,
accessible front-end layer that any ward office can operate without requiring special-
ized training or infrastructure investment.
     The platform is designed specifically for the Jaipur context, using real zone
coordinates and DLC rates applicable to Jaipur Nagar Nigam’s jurisdiction. Future
iterations could extend the system to other Rajasthan municipalities with minimal
configuration changes.

2.1       Overall Description

  This Software Requirement Specification (SRS) lays out exactly what the SMPTMS
system—developed for Jaipur Nagar Nigam—needs to do, covering both what it
must accomplish (the functional bits) and how it should perform (the non-functional
stuff). Here, you’ll get a sense of the bigger picture: how the system fits in, who’s
going to use it, any rules or constraints we have to work under, and the main assump-
tions behind how everything’s supposed to work. SMPTMS is a web application that
handles property tax management and controls access via different user roles. It uses
a three-layer setup: the Next.js frontend, business logic through Next.js API routes,
and a PostgreSQL database powered by Supabase.

2.1.1     Product Perspective

2.1.1.1   System Interfaces

  SMPTMS connects with three outside systems. Supabase is the main backend—it
manages all the data storage, live updates, and user authentication, all through its
PostgreSQL database. The application talks to Supabase using REST APIs over
HTTPS to keep things secure. For handling payments, everything goes through Ra-
zorpay. It supports both sandbox mode for testing and production mode for real pay-
ments. The GIS dashboard—basically, the map interface—gets its base layer from
Google Maps tile servers, and Leaflet.js renders the final map view users see. Data
is sent around as JSON, always over secure HTTPS, so everything stays compatible
and safe no matter where you access it.

  There are two main user journeys here, split by role. Citizens log in to find a

System Design Specification

3.1     System Architecture

  SMPTMS follows a three-tier client-server architecture. The presentation layer is
built on Next.js 14 with the App Router, running in the user’s browser as a React-
based single-page application. The application layer handles all business logic —
including tax calculation, authentication middleware, payment processing, and web-
hook verification — through Next.js API routes and server components executing in
a Node.js runtime environment. The data layer resides on Supabase, which manages
a PostgreSQL relational database, file storage for receipt PDFs, and real-time data
subscriptions.

  All three tiers communicate exclusively over HTTPS. Citizens and administrators
access the same Vercel deployment at smptms.vercel.app but are presented with en-
tirely different interfaces based on their assigned role, which is verified on every
request through Supabase Auth middleware running at the edge.

  The payment flow operates through a dedicated sub-process: when a citizen initi-
ates payment, the Next.js API creates a Razorpay order object, the citizen completes
payment on Razorpay’s hosted checkout page, and Razorpay sends a signed web-
hook callback to the server. The server verifies the cryptographic signature before
updating the property payment status in Supabase and generating the receipt docu-
ment.

Methodology and Team

4.1    Introduction to Agile Methodology

  Agile methodology breaks down project management into short, repeating cy-
cles—these are called sprints. Instead of waiting until the very end to deliver a
finished product, Agile teams roll out working pieces of the project bit by bit. This
lets everyone gather feedback, make changes, and steadily improve things the whole
way through.
      With SMPTMS, we went with Agile because we had four modules being de-
veloped at the same time, each by a different team member. This setup needed a lot
of coordination—people had to keep checking their work against each other’s and
make sure nothing was out of sync. Working in sprints allowed us to react quickly
when testing showed something needed to change. We could adjust requirements
and shuffle priorities at the end of each cycle instead of waiting for problems to pile
up.

  Here’s what the Agile phases looked like for SMPTMS:

  1. Requirement Gathering: The team started by digging into Jaipur Nagar Nigam’s
      property tax challenges. We also looked at other city platforms like PCMC
      Pune and BBMP Bengaluru. This research helped us list both the must-haves
      and nice-to-haves, which went into the SRS (Software Requirements Specifi-
      cation). Things like GIS map integration, Razorpay payments, and role-based
      access all ended up at the top of our priorities.

  2. Design: Next, we sketched out the system’s overall structure—this included the
      database setup (using Supabase PostgreSQL), API routing with Next.js App
      Router, and various design diagrams like use case, activity, DFD, class, and


Department of Computer Science and Engineering (AI), SKIT, Jaipur                   16
     sequence. We locked in a three-tier architecture, which made it easier to split
     the work into four separate modules for parallel development.

  3. Development (Coding): Each team member tackled their own module—and
     kept cycling through improvements. Khushal Jangid handled authentication
     and the user dashboard. Gaurav Pratap Singh built the tax calculation engine.
     Kapil Jarwal managed testing systems and set up the deployment pipeline. Hi-
     manshu Meena focused on GIS mapping and survey tools. We synced up every
     week to make sure all our parts worked nicely together.

  4. Testing: Testing happened step by step—first with unit tests, then integration
     and system checks. We relied on Postman for API testing and also did manual
     browser testing to make sure our UI was smooth. You’ll find all our test cases
     for each module in Chapter 5.

  5. Deployment: Once everything was ready, we launched the application on Ver-
     cel’s cloud. Continuous deployment was tied in with our GitHub repo, so up-
     dates went live fast. Supabase provided our managed PostgreSQL backend,
     and we set up Razorpay with both test and live payment credentials.

  6. Maintenance: After deployment, the team kept an eye on Vercel logs and
     Supabase query performance to catch and fix any runtime bugs quickly. All
     issues found in final testing were tracked and patched up before we submitted
     the project report.

System Testing

5.1     Introduction

  Software testing is the systematic process of evaluating a system to verify that it
behaves correctly and meets its specified requirements. For SMPTMS, testing was
conducted across three levels — unit, integration, and full system — to validate
every component before the platform was made available for live use. The primary
testing tool was Postman for API-level testing, supplemented by manual browser-
based testing for UI workflows and Razorpay’s sandbox environment for payment
validation.


5.2     Types of Testing Performed

5.2.1   Unit Testing

  Each module was tested independently before integration. The tax calculation en-
gine was tested by running a range of input combinations — varying plot sizes, zone
categories, and DLC rates — through the formula and verifying computed outputs
against manual calculations. The authentication module was tested by attempting
valid and invalid login credentials, checking session expiry behaviour, and validat-
ing role switching between citizen and admin accounts. Each API endpoint was
tested in isolation using Postman to confirm correct status codes, response payloads,
and error handling.

5.2.2   Integration Testing

  Integration testing focused on verifying correct data flow between modules. Key
integration test scenarios included confirming that a property registered by a citizen
appeared immediately on the admin GIS map with the correct zone and status, ver-

Department of Computer Science and Engineering (AI), SKIT, Jaipur                  21
ifying that a completed Razorpay payment updated the property record in Supabase
in real time via the webhook, and ensuring that the receipt generation function cor-
rectly pulled transaction details from the payment record. Cross-module interactions
between the user management, tax calculation, and payment modules were validated
in this phase.

5.2.3   System Testing

  Full end-to-end testing was performed on the live Vercel deployment. Test sce-
narios covered the complete citizen workflow: account registration, property sub-
mission, tax calculation review, Razorpay sandbox payment completion, and receipt
download. Admin workflows were also tested: logging in to the admin panel, view-
ing the GIS property map, filtering by zone and payment status, accessing the de-
faulter list, and generating zone-wise reports. All tests were repeated after bug fixes
to confirm resolution.

Conclusion and Future Scope

7.1    Conclusion

  The Smart Property Tax Management System (SMPTMS) proves you don’t need
expensive gear or complicated tech setups to build a smooth, transparent property
tax platform. By pulling together Next.js 14, Supabase, Leaflet.js, and Razorpay,
the project tackles a real problem: messy municipal tax collection in hundreds of
Indian cities.

  In Jaipur, the platform brings the entire property tax process online. Now, anyone
can register their property and pay taxes in just three minutes from any device with
a browser. The tax calculation is fixed, open for everyone to see, and used across all
city zones. That means there’s no room for the old manual tricks that used to chip
away at public trust in municipal tax systems.

  Admins get a GIS-based property map showing, in real time, who’s paying and
who isn’t. This helps them make smarter choices about enforcement and revenue
planning. All four modules are up, running, and tested at smptms.vercel.app. The
main goal? Cut out confusion, make it easy for people to pay, and rebuild trust in
property tax collection for smaller Indian cities. SMPTMS does exactly that.


7.2    Future Scope

  There’s plenty of room to make SMPTMS even better:

   • Link it up with Rajasthan’s ULBHRMS platform so all city-level records sync
      automatically. This cuts down on duplicate entries and gives a big-picture look
      at state-wide revenue.

     dues, easy access to payment history, and offline viewing of property details.

   • Use AI and satellite images to spot property anomalies in growing parts of
     Jaipur — catching undervalued or unregistered properties before they slip through
     the cracks.

   • Add support for Hindi and Rajasthani dialects to help property owners in rural
     areas who aren’t as tech-savvy.

   • Integrate WhatsApp Business API for automated payment reminders and in-
     stant tax receipt PDFs, making it super easy for citizens to get what they need.

   • Broaden the platform to include other municipal payments like trade licenses,
     water bills, and building plan approval fees — all from one portal.
